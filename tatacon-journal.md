What the heck is an ebox? it sounds like a crappy knock off of a famous video game console. But it has nothing to do with that, an ebox for Taiko no tatsujin is what houses the electronics and circuitry for your actual taiko controller. Sounds simple enough right?, but its quite the opposite an ebox is the most integral component of your drum. They can be both simple and complex, it all depends on your code  you can add acessiories and all sorts of stuff, at its core its just the controller to send inputs to a drum but it can be so much more dpeending on what you do with it.

### Content

Today, quite the interesting day honestly. I didn't expect to get that much time in today and was planning on just trying to see what I did wrong in my build. However, after some careful thinking, I realized that the problems could be boiled down to a couple things. First, my sensitivity thresholds on my coding were too high, causing an input to not register, but then I thought about it further. Why the heck wasn't ANYTHING coming through the signal, no matter if I tapped the piezos directly or shuck the wood block it was attached to. I realized that it could also be a hardware issue, because my piezos looked likle they had been through 2 and a half wars. They were dirty, parts broken and overall just yikes. I assumed this didn't change much but I decided to give it a go on the multimeter and compare with some extra piezos I had recently ordered that I was hestiating on opening. Well the difference was SHOCKING to give a pun. The piezos I had been using barely got up to 1 volt max and that was only one. Most of them were capping around .5v or less. This was terrible, especially compared to the new ones which went up to 5 or 6 pretty easily. I knew that I had to make some changes, so I desoldered the wire on my other piezos, and at first I thought it would be good to directly attach them to the new piezos. However, that turned out to be a mistake, because it damaged the piezos and it became ineffective just like my other ones. So instead, I attached to the wires that were already soldered onto the piezos. I was having some trouble at first, both because my soldering iron was kinda crappy(broken tip), and my technique is pretty awful. But I realized that the broken shape of my iron was actually prefect for putting solder onto wires, and after that it all came out pretty well. It was a very long process, stripping wires with scissors and an exacto knife, wrapping, then hot gluing the solder pad on the piezo, and doing that 8 times took me a while. However, I'm very happy with how they turned out, and I can safely say that they look a lot better, so hopefully my problems are solved (Atleast hardware wise, and I can move on to tweaking the code and testing later tonight).
